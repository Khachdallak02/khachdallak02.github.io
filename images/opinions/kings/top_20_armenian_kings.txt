================================================================================
                  TOP 20 ARMENIAN KINGS — BY HISTORICAL RECORD
   Selected from the Codex of 30 by depth of surviving primary sources and
   chronicle coverage.  Listed chronologically, from Hellenistic antiquity to
   the fall of Cilician Armenia.
================================================================================

CRITERION
  Each king below has either a contemporary or near-contemporary chronicler
  who covers him in detail (Pavstos Buzand on Arshak II, Agathangelos on
  Tiridates III, Thomas Artsruni on Gagik of Vaspurakan, Kirakos Gandzaketsi
  on Hethum I, French royal chronicles on Levon V, and so on) — or, for the
  ancients, substantial coverage in Greco-Roman historians (Plutarch, Tacitus,
  Strabo, Ammianus Marcellinus).

DROPPED FROM THE ORIGINAL 30 (sparsely documented)
  Orontes I, Orontes IV, Tigranes I, Tigranes III, Artaxias II,
  Khosrov III Kotak, Tiran, Abas I, Smbat II, Oshin.

================================================================================
                                ANTIQUITY
================================================================================

#01  ARTAXIAS I  (Artashes I)
     Dynasty :  Artaxiad
     Reign   :  c. 190–160 BC
     Era     :  Late Hellenistic

     Founder of the Artaxiad dynasty after the Seleucid collapse at Magnesia.
     Founded the capital Artaxata on the Araxes; Plutarch reports that the
     exiled Hannibal himself helped lay out the city at his court.  Set up
     bilingual Aramaic boundary stones across Greater Armenia — several of
     which have actually been excavated.

     Sources :  Strabo, Polybius, Plutarch (Lucullus, Life of Hannibal),
                Movses Khorenatsi.


#02  TIGRANES II "THE GREAT"
     Dynasty :  Artaxiad
     Reign   :  95–55 BC
     Era     :  Late Hellenistic

     Forged the largest empire in Armenian history — from the Caspian to the
     Mediterranean — and assumed the title King of Kings.  Founded the new
     capital Tigranocerta.  Allied with his father-in-law Mithridates VI of
     Pontus against Rome; defeated by Lucullus at Tigranocerta (69 BC) and
     forced to submit to Pompey in 66 BC, but retained his throne as a Roman
     client.  The apex of Armenian power.

     Sources :  Plutarch (Lucullus, Pompey), Cicero (Pro Lege Manilia),
                Strabo, Justin, Cassius Dio, Appian, abundant coinage.


#03  ARTAVASDES II
     Dynasty :  Artaxiad
     Reign   :  55–34 BC
     Era     :  Late Hellenistic / Late Roman Republic

     A learned monarch who wrote tragedies, histories and orations in Greek
     (Plutarch cites them).  Trapped between Rome and Parthia; he abandoned
     Mark Antony during the disastrous Parthian campaign of 36 BC.  In 34 BC
     Antony lured him to camp under safe-conduct, paraded him in golden
     chains in Alexandria, and Cleopatra had him beheaded.

     Sources :  Plutarch (Antony, Crassus), Cassius Dio, Strabo,
                Velleius Paterculus.


#04  TIRIDATES I
     Dynasty :  Arsacid
     Reign   :  63–88 AD
     Era     :  Early Roman Empire / Parthian era

     Founder of the Arsacid line in Armenia and a Zoroastrian magus.  His
     accession was the heart of the Roman–Parthian settlement of 63 AD that
     ended decades of war.  In 66 AD he travelled overland to Rome with a
     retinue of 3,000 to be crowned by Nero in a spectacular ceremony in the
     Forum — one of the most theatrically described episodes in Tacitus.

     Sources :  Tacitus (Annals 13–16), Cassius Dio, Pliny the Elder,
                Suetonius.


#05  TIRIDATES III "THE GREAT"
     Dynasty :  Arsacid
     Reign   :  c. 298–330 AD
     Era     :  Late Antiquity

     The king who in 301 AD made Armenia the first Christian state, converted
     by St. Gregory the Illuminator after a celebrated dynastic crisis.
     Raised at the Roman court before recovering his throne with Diocletian's
     help.  Famed in tradition for legendary physical strength.  His reign is
     the founding event of Armenian Christian historiography.

     Sources :  Agathangelos (his entire History of the Armenians),
                Movses Khorenatsi, Pavstos Buzand, Eusebius (indirectly).


#06  ARSHAK II
     Dynasty :  Arsacid
     Reign   :  c. 350–368 AD
     Era     :  Late Antiquity

     The defiant king of the long Armenian wars against Shapur II of Persia.
     Founded the refuge-city Arshakavan for fugitive peasants; clashed with
     his own nakharars and the Church; eventually lured by Shapur to a parley
     under safe-conduct, imprisoned in the Castle of Oblivion (Anhush) in
     Khuzestan, and died there.  Pavstos Buzand devotes his entire Book IV
     to this reign — one of the most fully narrated Armenian kings.

     Sources :  Pavstos Buzand (Book IV), Ammianus Marcellinus,
                Movses Khorenatsi.


#07  PAP
     Dynasty :  Arsacid
     Reign   :  370–374 AD
     Era     :  Late Antiquity

     Restored Armenian autonomy after his father Arshak II's imprisonment.
     Curbed the rising power of the Armenian Church (executed Catholicos
     Nerses, by tradition).  The Romans, alarmed by his independent streak,
     invited him to a banquet at the camp of Count Trajanus near Bagavan and
     assassinated him in 374 — the murder is narrated in granular detail by
     Ammianus Marcellinus.

     Sources :  Ammianus Marcellinus (Book XXX), Pavstos Buzand.


#08  VRAMSHAPUH
     Dynasty :  Arsacid
     Reign   :  389–417 AD
     Era     :  Late Antiquity

     A peaceful, learned king whose reign is remembered for one civilisational
     event: c. 405 AD, his courtier Mesrop Mashtots — supported by him and
     by Catholicos Sahak Partev — created the Armenian alphabet, opening the
     door to a national literature.  Koryun's Life of Mashtots is the
     contemporary witness to this royal patronage.

     Sources :  Koryun (Life of Mashtots), Movses Khorenatsi,
                Lazar Parpetsi.

================================================================================
                            MEDIEVAL BAGRATID ERA
================================================================================

#09  ASHOT I "THE GREAT"
     Dynasty :  Bagratid
     Reign   :  884–890
     Era     :  Early Medieval

     Restored the Armenian crown after more than four centuries of vassalage
     to Sasanians, Arabs and Caliphate.  Crowned with regalia sent by both
     the Abbasid Caliph al-Mu'tamid and the Byzantine Emperor Basil I — a
     uniquely bi-imperial recognition.  Founded the royal authority of the
     Bagratuni dynasty.

     Sources :  Yovhannes Drasxanakertc'i (John the Catholicos),
                Stepanos Asoghik.


#10  SMBAT I "THE MARTYR"
     Dynasty :  Bagratid
     Reign   :  890–914
     Era     :  Medieval

     Expanded the realm and contested Caucasia with the Sajid emirate, but
     was eventually defeated, captured and tortured to death by Emir Yusuf
     ibn Abi'l-Saj, who hung his body from the walls of Dvin — earning him
     the epithet "the Martyr".  Recorded by his contemporary, Catholicos
     Yovhannes, who lived through the events.

     Sources :  Yovhannes Drasxanakertc'i (eyewitness Catholicos),
                Stepanos Asoghik, Thomas Artsruni.


#11  ASHOT II "THE IRON"
     Dynasty :  Bagratid
     Reign   :  914–929
     Era     :  Medieval

     Drove out the Sajid armies in a long, brutal counter-offensive and was
     recognised as Shahanshah ("King of Kings of Armenia") by Caliph
     al-Muqtadir.  A reign of relentless warfare and restoration; the byname
     "the Iron" reflects both his persistence and a contemporary reputation
     for personal toughness in the field.

     Sources :  Yovhannes Drasxanakertc'i, Stepanos Asoghik.


#12  GAGIK ARTSRUNI
     Dynasty :  Artsruni  (Kingdom of Vaspurakan)
     Reign   :  908–943
     Era     :  Medieval

     Founded the independent Kingdom of Vaspurakan around Lake Van, in
     parallel with Bagratid Armenia, and broke decisively from Caliphal
     authority.  Built the Church of the Holy Cross on Aghtamar Island
     (915–921) — one of the supreme monuments of medieval Armenian
     architecture, whose exterior reliefs include the king himself holding a
     model of the church.

     Sources :  Thomas Artsruni (whose entire History of the House of
                Artsrunik' centres on this dynasty), the surviving Aghtamar
                inscriptions and reliefs.


#13  ASHOT III "THE MERCIFUL"
     Dynasty :  Bagratid
     Reign   :  953–977
     Era     :  Medieval

     Moved the Bagratid capital to Ani and earned his epithet through public
     works: founded monasteries, hospitals, leper-houses and almshouses.
     Tradition holds that he refused to dine without first inviting the poor
     to his table.  Ani begins its rise to "city of a thousand and one
     churches" under him.

     Sources :  Stepanos Asoghik, Aristakes Lastiverttsi, Matthew of Edessa.


#14  GAGIK I
     Dynasty :  Bagratid
     Reign   :  989–1020
     Era     :  Medieval (Bagratid zenith)

     The long, stable, prosperous reign at the apex of Bagratid Armenia.
     Patron of architecture and learning; under him Ani's cathedral (begun
     by Trdat the Architect under Smbat II) was completed, and the city's
     population is estimated at well over 100,000.  A great-power monarch
     among Byzantium, the Caliphate and the rising Seljuks.

     Sources :  Stepanos Asoghik, Aristakes Lastiverttsi, Matthew of Edessa.


#15  GAGIK II
     Dynasty :  Bagratid
     Reign   :  1042–1045
     Era     :  Medieval (Bagratid twilight)

     The last Bagratid king of Ani.  As a teenager he was lured to
     Constantinople under safe-conduct by Emperor Constantine IX
     Monomachos, pressured to abdicate, and held in gilded exile in
     Cappadocia, where he was eventually murdered by Byzantine Greek lords.
     Ani fell to Byzantium soon after — and to the Seljuks in 1064.

     Sources :  Aristakes Lastiverttsi (eyewitness), Matthew of Edessa,
                John Skylitzes.

================================================================================
                       THE ARMENIAN KINGDOM OF CILICIA
================================================================================

#16  LEVON I "THE MAGNIFICENT"  (Leo I/II of Cilicia)
     Dynasty :  Rubenid
     Reign   :  1198–1219  (prince from 1187)
     Era     :  High Middle Ages

     First crowned King of the Armenian Kingdom of Cilicia, in the cathedral
     of Tarsus on 6 January 1198 — under the aegis of the Holy Roman Empire
     (with Henry VI's crown delivered by the Archbishop of Mainz) and
     simultaneously recognised by Pope Celestine III and the Byzantine
     Emperor.  A master diplomat among Crusader states, the HRE, Byzantium,
     the Papacy and the Seljuks.

     Sources :  Smbat Sparapet's Chronicle, the Continuation of William of
                Tyre, Armenian and Latin Crusader chronicles, his own
                bilingual charters.


#17  HETHUM I  "THE MONGOL ENVOY"
     Dynasty :  Hethumid
     Reign   :  1226–1270
     Era     :  High Middle Ages

     In 1254 he travelled the entire length of Asia in person to meet
     Möngke Khan at Karakorum, sealing a formal Cilician–Mongol alliance
     that bought Cilicia decades of survival against the Mamluks.  Kirakos
     Gandzaketsi devotes a long, vivid chapter to the journey.  Grousset
     called him "one of the most lucid political geniuses of the Middle
     Ages."

     Sources :  Kirakos Gandzaketsi (detailed Karakorum journey), Smbat
                Sparapet, Het'um of Korykos (La Flor des Estoires),
                Matthew Paris.


#18  LEVON II  (Leo III of Cilicia)
     Dynasty :  Hethumid
     Reign   :  1269–1289
     Era     :  High Middle Ages

     Cilicia's longest reign of the late period and its cultural-economic
     golden age, even as Mamluk pressure intensified.  Patron of Toros
     Roslin's manuscript workshop, whose illuminated gospels survive and
     include royal portrait miniatures.  Strengthened trade ties with Genoa
     and the Mongol Ilkhanate.

     Sources :  Smbat Sparapet's Chronicle and continuators,
                the Roslin Gospels colophons.


#19  HETHUM II  "THE FRIAR-KING"
     Dynasty :  Hethumid
     Reign   :  1289–1307
     Era     :  High Middle Ages

     Abdicated and returned to the throne three times, took Franciscan vows,
     and ruled in tandem with his brothers and his young nephew Levon III.
     Erudite and pious — pushed for ecclesiastical union with Rome — but
     politically unstable; murdered together with King Levon III by the
     Mongol governor Bilarghu near Anazarbus in 1307.  His nephew Het'um of
     Korykos wrote about his court directly.

     Sources :  Het'um of Korykos (La Flor des Estoires d'Orient),
                Step'anos Orbelian, Latin and Franciscan chronicles.


#20  LEVON V  (Leo V/VI of Cilicia)
     Dynasty :  Lusignan
     Reign   :  1374–1375
     Era     :  Late Middle Ages

     The last king of Armenia.  Crowned in Sis as the Mamluks closed in;
     when Sis fell in 1375 he was captured and held in Cairo until ransomed
     by the Castilian and French courts.  Spent his final years in Paris as
     an honoured exile of Charles VI, even trying to mediate the Hundred
     Years' War.  Died in Paris in 1393 and was buried at the basilica of
     Saint-Denis among the kings of France — the only Armenian sovereign
     entombed in a Western royal cathedral.

     Sources :  Froissart's Chroniques, the Chronique du Religieux de
                Saint-Denis, papal letters, his Saint-Denis tomb effigy.
