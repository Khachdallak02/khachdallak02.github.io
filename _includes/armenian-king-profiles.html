Artaxias I B tier

Արտաշես Ա · Արտաշեսյան

Artaxiad · c. 190–160 BC

Founder of the Artaxiad dynasty who unified Greater Armenia, founded Artaxata (Plutarch credits Hannibal with helping lay out the city), and set bilingual boundary stones across the realm.

  • Diplomacy (13): Held the kingdom together after Seleucid collapse without overplaying his hand against Rome or Parthia.
  • Martial (15): Won the wars that made independence possible; a founder-king who fought as well as ruled.
  • Stewardship (16): Capital-building and territorial organisation are his main legacy — the strongest of his five stats.
  • Intrigue (10): Sources praise state-building more than court plots.
  • Learning (13): Hellenistic patronage and lawgiving, but not a scholar-king.

Why B tier: Total 67 — a capable founder with excellent stewardship, but not a empire-scale figure like Tigranes.

Tigranes II S tier

Տիգրան Բ · Արտաշեսյան

Artaxiad · 95–55 BC

King of Kings who forged the largest Armenian empire — Caspian to Mediterranean — built Tigranocerta, and only yielded to Rome after Lucullus and Pompey broke his outer provinces.

  • Diplomacy (17): Alliances with Pontus, Parthia, and Judea; survived as client king after defeat.
  • Martial (18): Decades of successful expansion before the Roman hammer fell — the apex of Armenian military power.
  • Stewardship (17): New capital, populated cities, and a treasury that matched his ambitions.
  • Intrigue (14): Managed a multi-ethnic court; not a plotter, but no fool either.
  • Learning (14): Hellenistic court culture at Tigranocerta, though war overshadows letters.

Why S tier: Total 80 — the only king here who clearly clears the “legendary” bar on multiple axes at once.

Artavasdes II A tier

Արտավազդ Բ · Արտաշեսյան

Artaxiad · 55–34 BC

A playwright-king who wrote tragedies in Greek, balanced Rome and Parthia for years, then was betrayed by Antony, paraded in golden chains, and executed by Cleopatra.

  • Diplomacy (14): Kept independence between two superpowers until the Antony disaster.
  • Martial (11): Abandoned Antony’s Parthian campaign — militarily cautious to a fault.
  • Stewardship (13): Maintained the realm through a dangerous era, without major building feats.
  • Intrigue (15): Survived by reading both empires; undone ultimately by trusting Antony’s safe-conduct.
  • Learning (18): Plutarch cites his Greek tragedies and histories — the highest Learning score in the list.

Why A tier: Total 71 — exceptional mind and solid diplomacy, dragged down by military caution and a catastrophic end.

Tiridates I B tier

Տրդատ Ա · Արշակունի

Arsacid · 63–88 AD

Founder of Arsacid Armenia and a Zoroastrian magus whose coronation by Nero in Rome — after a journey with 3,000 retainers — is one of Tacitus’s great set-pieces.

  • Diplomacy (16): The Nero coronation settlement ended decades of Roman–Parthian war over Armenia.
  • Martial (13): Magus first, general second; power came from negotiation.
  • Stewardship (13): Established a dynasty that lasted centuries.
  • Intrigue (12): Navigated Parthian and Roman interests without major scandal.
  • Learning (14): Religious learning as a magus counts here.

Why B tier: Total 68 — a diplomatic masterpiece, but not a builder or conqueror on Tigranes’s scale.

Tiridates III B tier

Տրդատ Գ · Արշակունի

Arsacid · c. 298–330 AD

Made Armenia the first Christian state (trad. 301 AD), converted by Gregory the Illuminator — the founding event of Armenian Christian historiography.

  • Diplomacy (14): Recovered his throne with Roman help and then steered a religious revolution.
  • Martial (16): Tradition credits him with legendary physical strength and battlefield prowess.
  • Stewardship (14): Laid the institutional groundwork for a Christian kingdom.
  • Intrigue (11): Agathangelos frames him as a convert, not a schemer.
  • Learning (13): Patron of the Church more than a scholar himself.

Why B tier: Total 68 — civilisational importance is unmatched, but the CK3 stats reflect a pious warrior-king rather than a universal genius.

Arshak II C tier

Արշակ Բ · Արշակունի

Arsacid · c. 350–368 AD

The defiant king of the long war against Shapur II; founded Arshakavan for refugees, clashed with nakharars and Church, and died in the Castle of Oblivion after a treacherous parley.

  • Diplomacy (10): Alliances failed; safe-conduct trap ended everything.
  • Martial (16): Pavstos Buzand’s whole Book IV — relentless resistance against Persia.
  • Stewardship (11): Arshakavan shows care for subjects, but the realm bled.
  • Intrigue (13): Court and Church conflicts; outplotted at the end.
  • Learning (10): Not remembered for scholarship.

Why C tier: Total 60 — heroic martyr-king whose martial valour could not compensate for diplomatic catastrophe.

Pap B tier

Պապ · Արշակունի

Arsacid · 370–374 AD

Restored autonomy after Arshak II’s imprisonment, curbed Church power, and was assassinated by the Romans at a banquet — narrated in granular detail by Ammianus Marcellinus.

  • Diplomacy (12): Played Rome and Persia until Rome decided he was too independent.
  • Martial (13): Fought to restore the crown; competent, not legendary.
  • Stewardship (12): Brief reign of recovery, cut short.
  • Intrigue (16): Executed Catholicos Nerses, defied Rome — then fell to a classic trap.
  • Learning (9): No scholarly reputation.

Why B tier: Total 62 — sharp and ruthless, but his story ends at a Roman dinner table.

Vramshapuh C tier

Վրամշապուհ · Արշակունի

Arsacid · 389–417 AD

Peaceful king whose reign is remembered for one civilisational event: Mesrop Mashtots created the Armenian alphabet (c. 405) with his patronage.

  • Diplomacy (13): Kept Armenia stable between Rome and Persia.
  • Martial (9): A reign of peace, not campaigns.
  • Stewardship (13): Patronage that enabled the alphabet — indirect but lasting.
  • Intrigue (8): Quiet court; little scheming recorded.
  • Learning (18): Alphabet patronage is the highest Learning event in Armenian history.

Why C tier: Total 61 — immortal through Mashtots, but personally a mild steward rather than a powerhouse ruler.

Ashot I B tier

Աշոտ Ա · Բագրատունի

Bagratid · 884–890

Restored the Armenian crown after centuries of foreign rule, recognised as king by both the Abbasid Caliph and Byzantine Emperor — the Bagratid restoration begins here.

  • Diplomacy (16): Dual recognition from Caliph and Emperor is exceptional.
  • Martial (14): Fought his way back to kingship after Arab domination.
  • Stewardship (14): Re-founded royal authority and dynastic legitimacy.
  • Intrigue (11): More restorer than plotter.
  • Learning (12): Standard medieval royal patronage.

Why B tier: Total 67 — indispensable restorer, but a short reign limits the score ceiling.

Smbat I C tier

Սմբատ Ա · Բագրատունի

Bagratid · 890–914

Expanded the realm against the Sajids but was captured, tortured, and hung from the walls of Dvin — the Martyr-king of Catholicos Yovhannes’s eyewitness account.

  • Diplomacy (13): Managed Caliphal politics until the Sajid crisis.
  • Martial (12): Won battles, then lost the war catastrophically.
  • Stewardship (12): Territorial gains undone by his capture.
  • Intrigue (9): Outmanoeuvred by Emir Yusuf.
  • Learning (11): No major cultural legacy beyond martyrdom.

Why C tier: Total 57 — memorable death, middling reign.

Ashot II C tier

Աշոտ Բ · Բագրատունի

Bagratid · 914–929

Drove out the Sajid armies in a brutal counter-offensive and was recognised as Shahanshah by the Caliph — “the Iron” for persistence in the field.

  • Diplomacy (12): Caliphal recognition capped the military victory.
  • Martial (17): The Iron — his byname is earned; highest martial focus among Bagratids.
  • Stewardship (11): War recovery, not golden-age building.
  • Intrigue (11): Straightforward warrior politics.
  • Learning (10): No scholarly epithet.

Why C tier: Total 61 — one-dimensional but effective war-king.

Gagik Artsruni A tier

Գագիկ Արծրունի · Արծրունի

Artsruni · 908–943

Founded the Kingdom of Vaspurakan and built the Church of the Holy Cross on Aghtamar — one of the supreme monuments of medieval Armenian architecture.

  • Diplomacy (14): Broke from Caliphal authority while surviving neighbours.
  • Martial (13): Held an independent kingdom by force and treaty.
  • Stewardship (17): Aghtamar and Vaspurakan’s institutions — builder-king.
  • Intrigue (11): Thomas Artsruni chronicles dynasty, not poison.
  • Learning (15): Patron of art and architecture at the highest level.

Why A tier: Total 70 — exactly on the A threshold; a complete medieval monarch.

Ashot III B tier

Աշոտ Գ · Բագրատունի

Bagratid · 953–977

The Merciful — moved the capital to Ani, founded hospitals and almshouses, and began Ani’s rise toward “the city of a thousand and one churches.”

  • Diplomacy (15): Kept Bagratid Armenia stable among Byzantium, Arabs, and Seljuks.
  • Martial (12): Peaceful reign; defence, not conquest.
  • Stewardship (17): Ani, public works, and charity — his defining stat.
  • Intrigue (10): Merciful, not Machiavellian.
  • Learning (14): Monastic and hospital patronage.

Why B tier: Total 68 — outstanding steward, but not a conqueror or universal monarch.

Gagik I A tier

Գագիկ Ա · Բագրատունի

Bagratid · 989–1020

Long, stable reign at the apex of Bagratid Ani — cathedral completed, city past 100,000, great-power diplomacy among Byzantium, the Caliphate, and the Seljuks.

  • Diplomacy (16): Three decades balancing the powers around Ani.
  • Martial (14): Deterrence and defence through strength, not expansion.
  • Stewardship (17): Ani’s golden age is his ledger.
  • Intrigue (12): Stable court; few recorded plots.
  • Learning (15): Architecture and learning flourished under him.

Why A tier: Total 74 — the best of the Ani Bagratids on every axis that matters for a medieval king.

Gagik II F tier

Գագիկ Բ · Բագրատունի

Bagratid · 1042–1045

Last Bagratid king of Ani — lured to Constantinople, forced to abdicate, murdered in exile. Ani fell to Byzantium, then the Seljuks.

  • Diplomacy (8): Walked into Byzantine safe-conduct and never walked out free.
  • Martial (10): Teen king with no great victories recorded.
  • Stewardship (8): Lost the capital and the dynasty.
  • Intrigue (7): Outplayed by Constantine IX Monomachos.
  • Learning (11): Slight edge as a educated prince, nothing more.

Why F tier: Total 44 — tragic figure, objectively the weakest reign on the list.

Levon I A tier

Rubenid · 1198–1219

First crowned King of Cilician Armenia — recognised by the Holy Roman Empire, Papacy, and Byzantium. Master diplomat among Crusaders, Latins, and Muslims.

  • Diplomacy (18): Highest diplomacy on the list — survival through recognition and alliance.

Hethum I A tier

Հեթում Ա · Հեթումյան

Hethumid · 1226–1270

Travelled to Karakorum in 1254 and allied Cilicia with the Mongols — shrewd realpolitik that bought decades against the Mamluks. Kirakos Gandzaketsi devotes a long chapter to the journey.

  • Diplomacy (17): Mongol alliance is the medieval diplomatic move of the century.
  • Martial (13): Competent commander; victory came from alliance.
  • Stewardship (14): Kept Cilicia viable under existential pressure.
  • Intrigue (15): Reading Mamluks, Mongols, and Crusaders simultaneously.
  • Learning (13): Well-travelled, informed ruler.

Why A tier: Total 72 — Grousset’s “political genius”; diplomacy and intrigue carry him.

Levon II A tier

Hethumid · 1269–1289

Cilicia’s cultural and economic golden age — patron of Toros Roslin’s manuscript workshop even as Mamluk pressure intensified.

  • Diplomacy (15): Trade with Genoa and the Ilkhanate; kept the peace where possible.

Why A tier: Total 70 — exactly on the A line; the last truly flourishing Cilician reign.

Hethum II C tier

Hethumid · 1289–1307

The Friar-King — abdicated and returned three times, took Franciscan vows, pushed union with Rome, and was assassinated with Levon III near Anazarbus.

  • Martial (10): Declining Cilicia; no martial glory.

  • Learning (16): Erudite, pious, Franciscan — high Learning, low everything else.

Why C tier: Total 59 — interesting man, poor king.

Levon V C tier

Լևոն Ե · Լուսինյան

Lusignan · 1374–1375

Last king of Armenia — Sis fell to the Mamluks; ransomed to France, died in Paris, buried at Saint-Denis among the kings of France.

  • Diplomacy (13): French and Castilian ransom networks; tried to mediate the Hundred Years’ War.
  • Martial (11): Lost the kingdom; fought a doomed defence.
  • Stewardship (9): Inherited collapse, could not reverse it.
  • Intrigue (11): Survived exile through connections, not schemes.
  • Learning (12): Cultured exile at the French court.

Why C tier: Total 56 — dignity in defeat, but the kingdom ended on his watch.
